People

Principal Investigator

Professor Raviraj Nataraj, Ph.D., is investigating innovative rehabilitation solutions to improve function following injury or neuromuscular disease. His current research interests include simulation-based development of exoskeleton controllers for gait, optimizing rehabilitation of hand function following stroke, and developing novel training paradigms to minimize musculoskeletal injury. He has previously performed clinical research involving restoration of standing following spinal cord injury at the Louis Stokes VAMC and investigation of hand sensorimotor control at the Cleveland Clinic. He has received postgraduate degrees in Mechanical and Biomedical Engineering from Stanford University and Case Western Reserve University.

Ph.D. Students

Sean Sanford

| Lab Manager, Ph.D. Candidate

Sean Sanford, M.S., joined the lab to pursue his Ph.D. degree after completing his Bachelor's degree in Biomedical Engineering from Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute in 2016. For his dissertation, Sean is creating novel multi-sensory feedback approaches for improving muscle control and increasing user-device integration. He is developing visual and haptic feedback methods to train consistent electromyography patterns for better control of a myoelectric assistive device. Sean is incorporating machine-learning algorithms that can accurately map user performance metrics, such as neurophysiological signals to device actions. Sean's concentration is in biomechanics and his interests are in rehabilitation engineering and athletic performance.

Mingxiao Liu

| Ph.D. Graduate Research Assistant

Mingxiao Liu, M.S., joined the lab to pursue his Ph.D. degree after completing his Masters's degree in Biomedical Engineering from Stevens Institute of Technology. For his dissertation project, he is optimizing rehabilitation of grasp control through cognitive agency. He is developing a prototype of a cognition glove to accelerate rehabilitation based on cognitive agency, and creating experimental methods for evaluating improvement in controlling a virtual hand prosthesis for patients with upper limb neuromuscular disorders. Mingxiao's concentration is in biomechanics and his interests are in rehabilitation engineering, device design and development. ​

Master's Students

Samuel Wilder

| 2020 BME High Academic Achievement Award Winner

Samuel Wilder, B.S., is an Electrical Engineering graduate student from Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute. He is investigating how to tune movement performance and speed in relation to the sense of agency in order to maximize performance of prosthetic movement. The study is tested in a virtual environment through devices in order to simulate minimal user input.​

He has joined the lab after completing his Bachelor's degree in Mechanical Engineering from The New York Institute of Technology in 2019. He is assisting in the research and development of an arm brace fixation device for the advancement of visual and haptic feedback methods to train users for better control of a myoelectric devices.
